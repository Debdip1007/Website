Internship Report

Characterization of Multiple Resonator Device for NV Center Decoherence Study
Student Name: Debdip Guchait

Aims

The project aims to design, fabricate, test, and implement multiple microwave superconducting resonators on a single chip, targeting the frequency range corresponding to spin transitions between the sub-levels of the triplet ground state around \( \mathrm{50~mT} \) of nitrogen-vacancy [\( \mathrm{NV^{-}} \)] centers in diamond. This aligns with the investigation of decoherence effects on the [\( \mathrm{NV^{-}} \)] centers in diamond due to nitrogen-related impurities, using a superconducting microwave resonator at millikelvin temperature.

Introduction & Background

In recent years defect centers in large bandgap crystals and superconducting circuit-based devices have become leading platforms in quantum information technology. \( \mathrm{NV^{-}} \) defect center in diamond is an important platform in quantum sensing, enabling microwave field spin manipulation, as a distinguishable single-photon source at optical wavelengths, as a memory element for quantum information, quantum-limited maser gain medium or a quantum transduction medium. \( \mathrm{NV^{-}} \) have been investigated rigorously for over two decades, making it one of the most studied defect centers, however a systematic study of factors limiting its spin coherence properties at low temperature is still lacking. In a recent article [1]🔗, researchers studied coherence properties of \( \mathrm{NV^{-}} \) at room temperature (RT), identifying nitrogen concentration within the diamond crystal as a limiting factor. We are interested in low temperature coherence properties and aim to investigate the coherence limits at milliKelvin temperatures.

In general EPR experiments, single spin coupling to AC magnetic fields is very low. To overcome the small sensitivity we work in the regime of cavity quantum electrodynamics. Here we use a resonator fabricated from a superconductor. The simplest resonator model is an inductor-capacitor (LC) oscillator. Its Hamiltonian can be modeled as a harmonic oscillator

\[ \hat{H} = \frac{\hat{\phi}^2}{2L} + \frac{\hat{q}^2}{2C} = \hbar \omega_0 \left(\hat{a}^{\dagger} \hat{a} + \frac{1}{2}\right) \]
where \( \hat{\phi} \) is the quantum operator for flux in the inductor, \( \hat{q} \) is the quantum operator for charge on the capacitor, \( \hat{a}^{\dagger}, \hat{a} \) are quantization operators such that \( \hat{N} = \hat{a}^{\dagger} \hat{a} \) is number of excitation in the oscillator. The induced current and voltage give rise to the associated electric \( \hat{\mathbf{E}}(r) = i\delta \mathbf{E}(r) (\hat{a}-\hat{a}^{\dagger}) \) and magnetic field \( \hat{\mathbf{B}}(r) = \delta \mathbf{B}(r) (\hat{a}+\hat{a}^{\dagger}) \), where \( \delta \mathbf{E}(r), \delta \mathbf{B}(r) \) are the root-mean-square (rms) vacuum fluctuation at position \( \vec{r} \) of the resonator. This vacuum fluctuations play a critical role in defining single spin-resonator coupling constant \( g_{0} \) and the Rabi frequency \( \Omega_s \). A higher \( g_0 \) value reduces the number of spins required to detect the spin magnetization.

In the electronic structure of \( \mathrm{NV^{-}} \), both the excited state and ground state are triplet spin state [spin quantum number \( S=1 \)], with a singlet [\( S = 0 \)] metastable state in between them. The ground state spin system has zero field splitting \( D/2\pi = 2.878 \) GHz between \( \ket{0} \) & \( \ket{\pm1} \) and a strain induced splitting \( E/2\pi = 1-5 \) MHz between \( \ket{1} \) and \( \ket{-1} \) [2]🔗. Under a strong magnetic field, we can restrict ourselves to a two-level system between \( \ket{0} \) & one of the \( \ket{\pm1} \). A key property of the \( \mathrm{NV^{-}} \) center is its ability to initialize the spin states to \( \ket{0} \) via optical excitation. The spin states can be manipulated using microwave (MW) excitation.

This spin-resonator system is used to transfer quantum information coherently to the spins. The interaction between a two-level system and the resonator can be modeled by Jaynes-Cummings form Hamiltonian

\[ \hat{H_{int}} = \gamma_{e} \mathbf{S} \cdot \mathbf{B} = g_{0} \left(\hat{a}\hat{\sigma_{+}}+\hat{a}^{\dagger}\hat{\sigma_{-}}\right) \]
where \( g_{0} = - \gamma_e {\delta \mathbf{B}} \cdot \mathbf{S} \), and \( \sigma_-, \sigma_+ \) are the Pauli spin ladder operation. The input-output theory allows us to extract information about the spin system from the reflection spectrum of the hybrid spin-resonator system [2]🔗.

Methods

In this project, superconducting resonators were nanofabricated in a cleanroom facility using maskless optical lithography, reactive ion etching, development, and spin coating and ultra-sonic cleaning. All the measurements at cryogenic temperature were conducted within a dilution refrigerator [DR] (Bluefors, LD400). We used a Keysight E5071C vector-network analyzer (VNA), for continuous wave (CW) microwave measurements, and Quantum Machine OPX+ for all pulse experiments. Simulations of the resonators were performed using RF module of COMSOL Multiphysics (finite-element method) and the Method-of-Moment based planar EM simulator Sonnet.

Design

To overcome the weak interaction between spin magnetic dipole moment and the magnetic field, a resonator with higher \( \delta \mathbf{B_0} \) at the spins is desirable. In a superconducting microwave resonator, the desired \( \delta \mathbf{B_0} \) can be easily obtained with a miniaturized resonator, which also allows integration of multiple spin samples in the limited sample space of the DR.

The choice of superconducting resonators introduces a few challenges in electron spin resonance ESR spectroscopy. In ESR spectroscopy, a DC magnetic field is applied to break the spin degeneracy and to match one of the spin transitions \( \omega_s \) to the resonance frequency, \( \omega_0 \), of the resonator. A low-loss, high-critical field superconductor is required for the resonator. The Meissner effect is the phenomenon of a magnetic field being expelled from the superconductor. In general, type-I superconductors have a low critical field \( H_c \), whereas type-II superconductors can possess a much higher upper critical field \( H_{c2} \). Of these type-II superconductors, Nb-based superconductors have some of the highest \( H_{c2} \) currently known. For this reason, we fabricated our resonators using \( \mathrm{Nb_x Ti_{1-x}N} \), a superconductor known for its high \( H_{c2} \).

In the free electron model, charge carriers having mass and velocity, carry kinetic energy. We can therefore define a kinetic inductance \( L_k \), where the \( K.E = \frac{1}{2} L_k I^{2} \). \( L_k \) induces an impedance when there is a change in current, similar to a geometrical inductance \( L_g \). We therefore need to characterize this inductance to match the characteristic impedance of the transmission line \( 50\Omega \) and desired resonance frequency. To do this, we first need to know the sheet inductance \( L_k/\square \) ('per-square kinetic inductance') of \( \mathrm{Nb_x Ti_{1-x}N} \) film. We estimate \( L_k/\square \) by measuring a few test quasi-fractal resonators of different sizes and determining their resonant frequency. We perform simulations of the same devices using Sonnet software and adjust \( L_k/\square \) until they match the experimental \( \omega_0 \) data. Details are given in the results section. The kinetic inductance per unit length is calculated as \( L_k / \text{length} = L_0 = \frac{L_k/\square}{w} \), where \( w \) is the wire width.

The internal quality factor \( Q_{i} \) of a superconducting microwave resonator depends on the losses due to external magnetic field \( Q_{i,B_0} \), two-level systems \( TLS \) in dielectric substrate \( Q_{i,die} \), ohmic loss \( Q_{i,ohm} \) and radiation loss \( Q_{i,rad} \). Dielectric loss can be reduced by improving the substrate with reduced two-level systems. In high \( \mathrm{T_c} \) superconductor operating at \( 12-20 \) mK, ohmic loss are negligible. Radiation loss increases when the phase velocity \( v_p \) of the electromagnetic field in the line exceeds that in the substrate, \( v_p = \frac{1}{\sqrt{lc}} \), where \( l = l_g + l_k \) is the inductance per unit length and \( c \) is the capacitance per unit length. An increase in \( l_k \) results in a reduced phase velocity in the line, which is achieved by reducing the wire width [3]🔗.

Conversely, an inhomogeneous microwave magnetic field across the resonator plane can cause variation in coupling to individual spins \( \omega_s \) placed on top of the resonator, leading to broader inhomogeneous linewidth and reduced free-induction decay time \( \mathrm{T_2^*} \). We engineer a homogeneous magnetic field at the spins being interrogated.

Our resonator designs are based on the quasi-fractal, notch type, tuning fork quarter wave resonator shown in Fig-1a. Each resonator consists of a \(U-\)shaped inductive element consisting of a \(2\mu m \) wide wire and an interdigitated capacitor located between the two prongs. The two prongs of the \(U-\)shaped inductor are separated by \(148\mathrm{\mu m}\). In the final measurement resonators, \(Q_{ext} \sim 800 \) was chosen to approximately match the inhomogeneous linewidth of ensemble \(\mathrm{NV^{-}}\) spins. The ground planes are separated from the resonator prongs by \(150\mathrm{\mu m}\), and are designed with quasi-fractal geometry to reduce flux focusing near the resonator, thereby improving the \(Q_{i}\) [4]🔗.

To estimate the kinetic inductance of the film, we designed test resonators of two different prong lengths \(l\), having different \(\omega_0\), summarized in the result section. Each chip was fabricated with \(100 \mathrm{nm}\) \(\mathrm{Nb_x Ti_{1-x}N}\). Each chip contains 10 resonators of different \(\omega_0\) and \(Q_{ext}\) to a transmission line. For the final measurement device, we have designed a chip with two transmission lines, each transmission line is used to interrogate five distinct resonators. The design is shown in fig-1d.

A new sample holder for the chip and PCB was designed to mount the sample on the mixing chamber plate of the DR. A PCB was designed to accommodate a maximum of eight transmission lines to interface mini-SMP connectors to the chip containing the resonators. The PCB and chip were placed inside a copper housing having \(10\) optic fiber hole \(\left( 500\mathrm{\mu m} \ \text{diameter}\right)\) to support optical access for the spin ensemble initialization and readout. The overall design is shown in fig-1e.

Fabrication

The resonators are fabricated on a \(300 \mathrm{\mu m}\) thick single-crystal \(3 \mathrm{in}\)-sapphire \(\left(\mathrm{Al2O3}\right)\) wafer [permittivity \(\epsilon_{r,\perp} = 11.34, \epsilon_{r,\parallel} = 9.34\) to the C-plane]. Before the evaporative deposition of the \(\mathrm{Nb_x Ti_{1-x}N}\), wafers are cleaned with isopropyl alcohol (IPA) and backed at \(105^oC\). The thickness of the deposited film by evaporation was controlled using a pre-calibrated deposition time \(58 \mathrm{s}\) for \(100\mathrm{nm}\). The evaporated wafer with \(\mathrm{Nb_x Ti_{1-x}N}\) spin-coated with OPA (Optical Photo-resist primer) and \(\mathrm{AZMIR703}\) as a positive optical photo-resist. The evaporated wafer was pre-backed for \(1\) min. The resonator pattern was exposed using standard maskless optical lithography with UV light using the Heidelberg Inst. MLA 150. The pattern was developed with AZ300 MIF for \(2\) min, rinsed with deionized water, and post-backed at \(105^o \mathrm{C}\) for 2 min to remove any water residue. Reactive ion etching (RIE) with \(\mathrm{{CF_4}}\) was used to etch the \(\mathrm{Nb_x Ti_{1-x}N}\) from the exposed regions of the chip. After the RIE, the residue resist mask was removed with ultra-sonication in Acetone, followed by IPA. The wafer was diced to individual chips. The resonator chips were wire bonded to the PCB and measured in the DR at \(12\mathrm{mK}\).

Results

COMSOL was used to simulate the vacuum electromagnetic field fluctuations of the resonator, a key parameter determining \( g_{0} \). The resonator of a target \( \omega_0 \) having \( Q_{ext} \approx 800 \) was designed with the help of Sonnet simulations based on the estimated \( L_k/\square \). Sapphire was defined as a block [\( 3\times 0.75\times0.3 \ \mathrm{mm} \)] of \( \epsilon_r = 11.34 \). A 2-D drawing of the resonator was imported onto the sapphire block. An air layer of thickness \( 1\mathrm{mm} \) was included to simulate the device environment. The vacuum EM field fluctuation distribution of the final device is shown in Fig-1b, [test resonator simulations are not shown here]. The resonator design is optimized to have a homogeneous magnetic field distribution sustained by the resonator at the center of the loop at the very top section. A map of effective \( g_{0} \) in the plane perpendicular the 2-D resonator is shown in fig-1c. The transmission lines used to interrogate each resonator were designed as \( 50\Omega \) CPWs. A chip with a diamond on top is placed within PCB cutout and enclosed in the copper housing, as shown in Fig-1f.

The resonator frequencies \( \omega_0 \) and external quality factor \( Q_{ext} \) for both test and final devices are summarized in Table 1. We use the QKit circle fit [5]🔗 in the imaginary plane to extract \( Q_{i} \), \( Q_{ext} \), and \( \omega_0 \) fig-2a and 2b. We estimate a \( Q_i \approx 5.34\times 10^5 \). We estimate \( L_k/\square = 1.85\mathrm{pH} \) for a \( 107\mathrm{nm} \) thick \( \mathrm{Nb_x Ti_{1-x}N} \) by repetitive simulation of one of the test resonators until the simulation \( \omega_0 \) matches the experimental result. Using the estimated value for the \( 107 \mathrm{nm} \) film, we estimate \( L_k/\square = 1.97\mathrm{pH} \) for \( 100\mathrm{nm} \) thickness superconducting film with the equation

\[ L_k/\square = \mu_0 \lambda \coth\left(\frac{t}{\lambda}\right) \]
[6]🔗 leads to the \( L_k = 985\mathrm{nH/m} \) for a \( 2\mathrm{\mu m} \) wide wire of \( 100\mathrm{nm} \). Increased power changes \( \omega_0 \) and \( Q_{ext} \) due to the nonlinearity of the kinetic inductance, and at the peak power, superconductivity starts to break due to the breaking of cooper pairs [not shown in the figure].

Under an external magnetic field, in the type-II superconductors vortices are generated around the penetrated field lines, and become a prominent loss mechanism. We show in fig-2c & 2d that under \( \vec{B}_0 \perp \) to the resonator, the resonator loss increases after \( 20\mathrm{mT} \), but with \( \vec{B}_0 \parallel \) to the resonator, the resonator is more robust.

Experimental Set-Up

The resonator is thermally anchored to the \(10\mathrm{mK}\) stage and is placed at the center of the magnetic field of the superconducting vector magnets, which is thermalized at \(4\)K. On each line, attenuator, low-pass filters and IR filters are installed. The input CW line A1 and the pulse line C2 are merged using a directional coupler at the \(10\mathrm{mk}\) mixing chamber plate and directed to one of the ports of the transmission line. The transmitted signal is directed through a double circulator to the readout line \(O1\) equipped with a high electron mobility transistor amplifier (HEMT), at \(4\)K plate and a cryogenic switch is installed to protect the HEMT from strong microwave signal that could damage the HEMT. The input/output of the VNA is connected to the DR CW input line A1 and output line O2 for CW measurements as shown in Fig-2e. The Cryo-microwave switch is kept open during CW measurements. The transmission spectrum of the resonator chip inside the DR is measured through an RT microwave amplifier.
